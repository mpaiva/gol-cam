## 1. Origins of the Sport in Brazil

### Early History

Futebol de botao (button football), also known officially as "futebol de mesa" (table football), emerged in Brazil in the early 20th century. The game evolved organically from children improvising tabletop football using household items -- actual clothing buttons, bottle caps, and other small circular objects -- to simulate real football matches on flat surfaces.

The sport is believed to have crystallized into a recognizable form in the 1930s, when Brazilian children and young men began standardizing the practice of flicking circular pieces across smooth surfaces to simulate the beautiful game. By the late 1940s and 1950s, the hobby had grown into a semi-organized pastime with informal clubs and neighborhood tournaments.

### Formalization

The first organized competition rules date to **1959**, when the "1 Toque" (one-touch) variant was codified, making it the oldest organized rule set in Brazilian button football. This marked the transition from informal play to structured sport.

The national governing body, the **Confederacao Brasileira de Futebol de Mesa (CBFM)**, was eventually established to oversee the sport across Brazil. The CBFM has maintained yearbooks (anuarios) since **1961**, documenting competitions across the country. The confederation currently oversees **27 state federations**, one for each Brazilian state and the Federal District.

The **Federacao de Futebol de Mesa do Estado do Rio de Janeiro** is the state-level body for Rio de Janeiro, currently presided by Claudio Pinho. It organizes competitions in six modalities: 1 Toque, 3 Toques, 12 Toques, Dadinho, Sectorball, and Subbuteo.

## 2. How the Game Is Played -- General Overview

### The Field

The game is played on a smooth, flat surface -- traditionally a large board or table -- painted or printed to resemble a miniature football pitch with markings for the center circle, penalty areas, goal areas, and corner arcs. Official tables have standardized dimensions. The goals are miniature structures placed at each end.

### The Buttons (Players)

Each team consists of **11 circular discs** (the "buttons" or "botoes"), typically 35-60mm in diameter. These represent the 11 players on a football team. Originally made from actual buttons, over the decades they evolved into specially manufactured pieces made from materials like bakelite, acrylic, bone, porcelain, and modern plastics. Collectors prize handmade buttons, and many are painted in the colors of real football clubs.

Each player also has a **goalkeeper**, which is a larger, rectangular piece or a specially shaped figure that can be manipulated to defend the goal.

### Basic Gameplay

Players take turns flicking their buttons with a finger to strike the ball piece and move it toward the opponent's goal. The core mechanic is:

1. A player flicks one of their buttons to hit the ball
2. If the button successfully contacts the ball, the player continues their turn
3. If the button misses the ball, or the ball touches an opponent's button, possession changes
4. The objective is to maneuver the ball into the opponent's goal

### The Ball Piece -- Where Variants Diverge

This is where the critical distinction between rule variants emerges. In most traditional rules, the ball is a **small flat disc** (a "balinha" or "pastilha"), typically smaller than the player buttons. However, in the **Dadinho** variant, the ball is a **small cube -- a tiny dice** (dado/dadinho).

## 3. The Major Rule Variants in Brazil

The CBFM recognizes the following official variants:

### 3.1 -- 1 Toque (One Touch)
- **Origin:** The oldest organized rule, since 1959
- Smooth or carved button designs
- High emphasis on technique and strategy
- One touch per button per turn

### 3.2 -- 3 Toques (Three Touches) -- "Regra Carioca"
- **Previously known as "Regra Carioca"** (the Carioca Rule, i.e., the Rio de Janeiro Rule)
- 40-minute matches
- Three-touch collective limit per possession
- Mandatory passing rules before shooting
- This is historically significant as it was the rule set developed and popularized in Rio de Janeiro

### 3.3 -- 12 Toques (Twelve Touches) -- "Regra Paulista"
- **Previously known as "Regra Paulista"** (the Sao Paulo Rule)
- 20-minute matches divided into two 10-minute halves
- Collective limit of 12 touches per possession
- Originated in Sao Paulo

### 3.4 -- Dadinho
- **The newest official rule**, approved by the CBFM in **2010**
- Uses a **cubic piece (the "dadinho")** instead of a flat disc as the ball
- The dadinho (cube) measures approximately **0.6cm per face** and weighs **0.1-0.3 grams**
- Player buttons are circular discs of **35-60mm diameter**
- **Individual touch limit:** Maximum 3 consecutive touches per single button
- **Collective touch limit:** Maximum 9 team touches total per possession
- By the 9th touch, the team must attempt a goal shot or face an indirect penalty
- Rule alterations were made effective May 1, 2024

### 3.5 -- Other Variants
- **Sectorball:** International rule adapted from European play
- **Chapas:** Spanish-origin rule popular in Sao Paulo and Rio de Janeiro
- **Subbuteo:** European variant using anthropomorphic figurines on a cloth field

## 4. The Dadinho Variant -- Deep Dive

### What is a "Dadinho"?

The word "dadinho" is the diminutive of "dado" (dice) in Portuguese. In the context of button football, it refers to a **small cube** used as the ball. The most iconic version is a **small yellow dice**, typically from board games or lottery kits, repurposed as the ball piece in this variant of the game.

### Origins and Cultural Context

The Dadinho variant represents an organic, grassroots evolution of button football. While the flat-disc ball variants were codified earlier (3 Toques in Rio, 12 Toques in Sao Paulo), many informal players -- particularly in Rio de Janeiro and surrounding states -- preferred using a small dice as the ball because:

1. **Availability:** Small dice were readily available from board games, easily found in any household
2. **Unpredictability:** The cubic shape introduces an element of controlled chaos -- the dadinho bounces and rolls differently than a flat disc, adding dynamism
3. **Speed of play:** Games with the dadinho tend to be faster and more action-oriented
4. **Cultural identity:** The dadinho became a marker of informal, street-level play as opposed to the more "official" flat-disc rules

The Dadinho variant was practiced informally for decades before the CBFM officially recognized it in **2010**, making it the newest addition to the federation's official rule portfolio. This recognition was a significant moment for practitioners who had been playing with the dadinho for generations.

### Gameplay Specifics

The Dadinho variant plays as follows:

- **Ball:** A small cube (dadinho), approximately 6mm per face, weighing 0.1-0.3 grams
- **Buttons:** Standard circular discs, 35-60mm diameter
- **Individual limit:** A single button may touch the dadinho a maximum of 3 consecutive times
- **Team limit:** The team has a collective maximum of 9 touches before they must shoot
- **Shot requirement:** By the 9th collective touch, a goal attempt is mandatory, or the team is penalized with an indirect free kick for the opponent
- **The cube factor:** Because the dadinho is cubic rather than flat, it behaves unpredictably when struck -- it can bounce, hop, spin, and tumble. This requires different technique compared to the flat ball variants and introduces a controlled element of chance

### Competitive Structure

As of 2025-2026, the Dadinho variant has a robust competitive structure:

- **Brazilian National Championship:** The 16th Brazilian Dadinho Championship was held in October 2025 (won by Gianluca)
- **State Championships:** Active competitions documented in Rio de Janeiro, Sao Paulo (Corinthians won the state title in October 2025), Rio Grande do Sul, Mato Grosso do Sul, Paraiba, Piaui, Santa Catarina, and other states
- **Team Championships:** Club-based team competitions (e.g., Rio de Janeiro team finals in December 2025)
- **Expansion:** The first Piaui State Dadinho Championship was held in January 2026, showing the variant continues to expand geographically

## 5. Rio de Janeiro's Role in Button Football Culture

### The Birthplace of "Regra Carioca"

Rio de Janeiro has a foundational role in Brazilian button football. The city gave its identity to the "3 Toques" variant, originally known as the **"Regra Carioca"** (Carioca Rule). This three-touch rule emphasizes passing and team play, reflecting the traditionally fluid and creative style of Carioca football culture. The "Historico do Campeonato Carioca 1978 a 2025" -- a historical record maintained by the CBFM -- documents nearly 50 years of Rio de Janeiro state championships.

### Cultural Significance

Button football in Rio de Janeiro is more than a game; it is embedded in the cultural fabric of the city:

- **Neighborhood culture:** The game has historically been played in bars, community centers, clubs, and homes across Rio's neighborhoods. It served as a social glue, bringing together people of different ages and backgrounds.

- **Football obsession extension:** In a city where football (soccer) is a near-religious experience -- home to Flamengo, Fluminense, Vasco da Gama, and Botafogo -- button football provided a way to continue living football off the real pitch. Players would create buttons painted in their favorite team's colors.

- **Intergenerational transmission:** The game was traditionally taught from father to son, uncle to nephew. It was a rite of passage for many Carioca boys.

- **Working-class roots:** Unlike more expensive hobbies, button football required minimal investment -- a smooth surface, some buttons or caps, and a small ball piece (or dadinho). This accessibility made it deeply democratic.

- **Collector culture:** A rich collecting culture developed around buttons, with artisans crafting beautiful hand-painted buttons representing every major football club. This artisanal tradition remains alive in Rio.

### The Dadinho Connection to Rio

While the Dadinho variant is now practiced nationally, its strongest cultural roots are in Rio de Janeiro and neighboring states. The informal, improvisational spirit of using a small dice as the ball aligns with the Carioca tradition of "jeitinho" -- the creative, adaptive Brazilian way of making things work with what is available. The dadinho was the ball of the streets, the ball of the improvised game on a kitchen table or a bar counter.

The Rio de Janeiro state federation actively organizes Dadinho tournaments, with team finals documented as recently as December 2025.

## 6. Notable Tournaments, Clubs, and Traditions

### Key Tournaments

- **Campeonato Brasileiro Individual de Dadinho:** National individual championship; the 16th edition was held in 2025
- **Campeonato Brasileiro de Equipes:** National team championship
- **Campeonato Carioca:** Rio de Janeiro state championship, documented from 1978 to present (nearly 50 years of continuous competition)
- **Campeonato Brasileiro Individual de 1 Toque:** Running since 1970
- **Campeonato Brasileiro Interclubes 3 Toques:** Running since 1981
- **Trofeu Atila Lisa:** A traditional named trophy competition (2026 edition documented)

### Notable Clubs

Button football in Brazil is organized around clubs, much like real football:
- **Corinthians** (Sao Paulo) -- Won the 2025 Sao Paulo State Dadinho Championship
- **Avai** (Santa Catarina) -- Won four consecutive state team titles in Dadinho
- **SpecialOne** -- Won the 5th Copa AMFM de Dadinho (2023)
- **AABB-LUCFM** (Rio Grande do Sul) -- Won the IV State Team Championship

Many of these clubs bear the names of real Brazilian football clubs, reflecting the deep connection between the tabletop and real-world football cultures.

### Traditions

- **Painting buttons in club colors:** A deeply held tradition where each player's set of buttons is carefully painted to represent their favorite real-world football club
- **Tournament circuits:** Regular competitive circuits at municipal, state, and national levels
- **Artisanal button making:** Craftsmen who specialize in creating high-quality buttons from various materials
- **Publication culture:** The CBFM maintains yearbooks since 1961 and has published works such as "O Futebol De Mesa" by Julio Diogo and Douglas Rambor

## 7. Summary of Key Findings

1. **Origins:** Futebol de botao emerged in Brazil in the early-to-mid 20th century from children using household buttons to simulate football. Formal organization began in 1959 with the 1 Toque rule.

2. **Rio de Janeiro is central** to the sport's identity, having originated the "Regra Carioca" (3 Toques) and maintaining nearly 50 years of documented state championships (1978-2025).

3. **The Dadinho variant** uses a small cubic dice (approximately 6mm per face) instead of a flat disc as the ball. It was played informally for decades before being officially recognized by the CBFM in 2010. It features a 3-touch individual limit and 9-touch team limit with mandatory shot requirement.

4. **The sport is alive and growing:** As of 2025-2026, new state championships are being established (Piaui held its first Dadinho championship in January 2026), and 27 state federations operate under the CBFM.

5. **Cultural significance:** Button football is a democratic, accessible sport deeply tied to Brazilian football culture, neighborhood social life, intergenerational bonding, and artisanal craftsmanship.
